SleePod

(Ongoing)

The SleepPod tracks and analyzes various sleep aspects to promote health and well-being. It monitors snoring, palpitations, hyperventilation, breathing lapses, and sleep disturbances, while optimizing the sleep environment by managing external factors. The hardware includes microphones, sound meters, respiratory and heart rate sensors, and environmental sensors for temperature, humidity, and noise. These components work with sound analytics software to process audio data. The device, placed on a nightstand, uses filtering techniques to minimize echo and stores audio files on a memory card or in real-time, transferring them to the cloud for analysis. Security is ensured through end-to-end encryption. The system focuses on sleep patterns, deprivation, and age-related factors to provide insights for improving sleep quality and well-being, though it is not for diagnostic purposes.

Smart Mirror

Mirror Mirror on the Wall - CSV

The project involves developing a smart mirror platform with a Raspberry Pi and a custom two-way mirror to provide an interactive hub for real-time information and hands-free functionality through Amazon's Alexa. The mirror will overlay digital content onto the reflection, creating a dynamic interface. The Raspberry Pi will handle data processing, API interactions, and the user interface.

API integrations such as Spotify for music and WeatherApp for weather forecasts will display real-time data on the mirror. Alexa's functionality will be enhanced to offer intuitive, context-aware interactions, providing a streamlined, voice-controlled experience that emphasizes ease of use and accessibility.

Music Player

In the circuit implementation, a voltage regulator converts the 9V battery voltage to a stable 5V output. The regulator's first pin connects to the positive terminal of the battery, the second pin to the negative terminal of a capacitor and the ground rail of the breadboard, while the third pin provides the 5V supply to the circuit. The microcontroller is powered by connecting its VCC pin to the positive terminal and its GND pin to the negative terminal of the breadboard.

The keypad is connected to Port C of the microcontroller, with the rows linked to pins PC0–PC3 and columns to pins PC4–PC7. Three 1000-ohm resistors connect between the negative terminal and the VO pin of the LCD. The LCD's RS, R/W, and EN pins connect to Port B0, B1, and B2 for control. Data passes through Port D's DB0–DB7 pins. A 0.1uF capacitor connects the speaker to the microcontroller via Port PB3. The keypad is used to select songs (A, B, C, D), and the MCU displays the song name on the LCD. Tempo and pitch keys adjust preferences, updating the notes played through the speaker.
